Purpose

Meetings are the heart and soul of democracy. They are where people of different opinions listen to each other and present their points of view.

In the Athenian Assembly of 500 BC, a quorum of 6,000 of the 43,000 citizens was needed to conduct business. In the 10th century, the Icelandic Althing was held in a natural outdoor amphitheater and all citizens could attend.

Today it is rare to see more than a dozen attendees at a council meeting in a town of 30,000 people. Is this still democracy? Some meetings are broadcast on local TV. But even the very few who watch cannot participate.

Sometimes, minutes are kept of meetings. But minutes don't record the most important part -- the passionate debate for and against proposed legislature.

Disenchantment with democracy starts at the small town community level. Even small town politicians know that an extremely small pecent of their voters watch what they do. For democracy to work, the majority of citizens need to be involved continually.

Democracy is never easy. But it was easier 2,000 years ago when only 50,000 need to work together. Today, millions need to do so. And each of us belongs to multiple goverments: for our town, our county, our state and country.

The tools for democarcy haven't changed much in 2,000 years. But today with technolgy, we finally are able to create tools that can allow millions to work together.

In 2018, there were 19,495 self governing cities and towns in the United States. How many exist throughout the world, 200,000, 300,000 or more?

How many of these governing bodies have open public meetings? And how many of their citizens are able to participate in them or even know what went on at them?

The purpose of Govmeeting is to build software that will enable many more people to participate in their governments. The goal is to build something generic enough that it can be used in any country on any leveland and in any form of participative government.

Most other "Open Government" projects rely on government supplied data. Govmeeting takes the opposite approach. It will use government data if available. But if government can't or won't supply the needed data, it provides a way for citizens themselves to create the data that it needs to monitor government actions.

The workings of democracy need to be learned first at the grassroot level - in towns, villages and small cities. That is also where it is easier for it to work as first envisioned. It is only if democracy can succeed at the grassroots level, that there is any hope of it succeeding at much higher levels.

Govmeeting's initial primary focus is on tools that work the best at the local level. Lessons learned there can help us fashion the tools we need at the state and country level.

Govmeeting is an open source project on Github.
